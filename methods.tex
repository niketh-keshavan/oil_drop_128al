\begin{abstract}

The elementary charge $e$ is a fundamental constant of physics which was first measured experimentally in 1909 by Robert Millikan through an experiment involving charged oil droplets suspended in an electric field. In our recreation of his experiment, we revisit this classical measurement by analyzing high-speed video recordings of oil droplet oscillations across three separate trials. Droplet velocities were extracted through frame-by-frame video analysis of the vertical oscillations between turning points, and the charge on each droplet was calculated using force balance equations with Cunningham slip correction applied to account for non-continuum effects at small particle sizes. Per-cycle charge analysis revealed that most droplets experienced mid-oscillation charge changes due to stray ionization, with the charge shifting by 1--3 elementary charges between consecutive oscillation cycles; in total, 20 distinct charge states spanning $n = 4$ to $n = 15$ were identified across the six recordings. Our mean-based analysis yields an elementary charge of $e = (1.603 \pm 0.051) \times 10^{-19}$~C, consistent with the accepted CODATA value to within 0.05\%, while the per-cycle charge-state analysis provides additional evidence for quantization by resolving discrete charge transitions at the single-electron level.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The elementary charge $e$ is one of the most precisely known fundamental constants in physics, yet its experimental determination remains a profound achievement in experimental physics. Unlike many physical quantities that vary continuously, electric charge is quantized, meaning it exists only in discrete multiples of a fundamental unit. The value $e = 1.60217663 \times 10^{-19}$ C represents the magnitude of charge carried by a single proton, and all observed electric charges in nature are integer multiples of this quantity.

The first precise experimental measurement of $e$ was obtained by Robert Millikan at the University of Chicago between 1909 and 1913. Although the discreteness of charge had been suspected since J.J. Thomson's identification of the electron, Millikan's oil drop experiment provided irrefutable evidence by directly observing the quantized nature of charge on individual droplets. His careful measurements not only confirmed the quantization hypothesis but also provided a value for $e$ accurate to within 0.6\% of the modern accepted value—a remarkable achievement given the experimental constraints of the era.

The fundamental principle underlying the Millikan oil drop experiment is the equilibrium of three forces acting on a small, charged oil droplet suspended between two parallel, oppositely charged plates. Consider a spherical droplet of radius $a$ and mass $m = \frac{4}{3}\pi a^3 \rho_{\text{oil}}$, where $\rho_{\text{oil}}$ is the mass density of mineral oil. This droplet is subject to three forces:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $F_g = mg$ (acting downward)
    \item $F_E = qE = \frac{qV}{d}$ (direction dependent on charge sign and field polarity)
    \item $F_d = 6\pi \eta a v$ (opposing the direction of motion)
\end{itemize}

where $q$ is the charge on the droplet, $V$ is the applied voltage between the plates, $d$ is the plate separation, $\eta$ is the dynamic viscosity of air, and $v$ is the instantaneous velocity. The drag force is governed by Stokes' law, valid for laminar flow around a spherical object.

At terminal velocity, the net force on the droplet vanishes, and the droplet moves at constant velocity. Two regimes of motion are observed: a \emph{falling phase} in which the electric field is turned \emph{off}, allowing gravity and drag to balance in the absence of an electric force, and a \emph{rising phase} in which the electric field is \emph{on} and the electric force overcomes gravity. In the falling phase, with the electric field disabled:
\begin{equation}
mg = 6\pi \eta a v_{\text{fall}}
\end{equation}

In the rising phase, with the electric field applied:
\begin{equation}
qE - mg = 6\pi \eta a v_{\text{rise}}
\end{equation}

By measuring both velocities and knowing the apparatus parameters, the charge $q$ can be solved for. The droplet radius $a$ is initially estimated from the falling velocity alone, using the condition that gravity balances drag. Subsequently, the Cunningham slip correction is applied to account for the breakdown of the continuum assumption at micrometer length scales.

For droplets with radii comparable to the mean free path $\lambda$ of air molecules (approximately 0.064 $\mu$m at standard conditions), the assumption that air behaves as a continuous medium begins to fail. Near the surface of the droplet, gas molecules no longer undergo perfectly elastic collisions with the droplet; instead, some molecules can traverse the region near the surface without colliding, leading to velocity slip. This causes the effective drag force to be smaller than predicted by Stokes' law.

The Cunningham slip correction factor accounts for this effect by modifying the drag coefficient:
\begin{equation}
F_d = \frac{6\pi \eta a v}{1 + \frac{b}{p a_1}}
\end{equation}

where $b = 8.226 \times 10^{-3}$ Pa$\cdot$m is the Cunningham constant, $p$ is atmospheric pressure, and $a_1$ is the droplet radius estimated from the falling velocity. The correction is typically expressed as multiplying the uncorrected charge by a factor:
\begin{equation}
q = \frac{q_0}{\left[1 + \frac{b}{p a_1}\right]^{3/2}}
\end{equation}

For droplets with $a_1 \sim 1$ $\mu$m, this correction increases the calculated charge by approximately 5--10\%, making it essential for accurate measurements.

In Millikan's original experiment, droplets were observed through a telescope with a reticle scale, and the vertical positions were recorded manually. In this modern iteration, we employ high-speed video recording to capture droplet oscillations with frame-level precision. 

We recorded video sequences at approximately 16-17 frames per second, then manually identified the frame numbers at which the droplet reaches its topmost and bottommost positions (the turning points). The time interval $\Delta t$ between consecutive turning points is determined from the frame rate and frame count, and the corresponding distance is read from the reticle scale visible in the video. Thus, the terminal velocity is simply:
\begin{equation}
v = \frac{\text{distance}}{\Delta t}
\end{equation}

Multiple oscillation cycles are recorded per droplet to obtain averaged velocities and to quantify measurement uncertainty through standard deviation calculations.

\section{Experimental Methods}

\subsection{Apparatus and Setup}

The fundamental apparatus used in this experiment is a commercial Millikan oil drop apparatus (PASCO AP-8210A), which provides a pair of horizontal parallel metal plates separated by a precisely machined distance of $d = 7.37$ mm. The plates can be charged to a controllable voltage, ranging from 0 to approximately 600 V. A thermistor embedded within the apparatus measures the environmental temperature, while a reticle scale visible through the observation telescope provides a spatial reference with each major division corresponding to 0.500 mm. The apparatus is mounted on a stable optical bench to minimize vibration during measurement.

The oil mist is generated by atomizing ordinary mineral oil (density $\rho_{\text{oil}} = 853$ kg/m$^3$) using a mechanical atomizer. Droplets enter the space between the parallel plates and become charged through frictional interactions during the atomization process. An ionizer can be activated to introduce additional charge onto existing droplets, increasing their net charge without changing their mass. The electric field is applied perpendicular to the gravitational field, with the field strength given by $E = V/d$, where $V$ is the applied voltage.

We used an external high-voltage power supply to drive the electric field between the plates. For the fall phase measurements, this power supply was disabled (0 V field), allowing gravity alone to drive the droplet downward. For the rise phase measurements, the power supply was set to maximum, which fell within within the range $V = 552$--555 V (nominal $V_{\text{nom}} = 553.5$ V). The voltage was monitored continuously during each trial to detect drift, with recorded values accurate to $\pm 1$ V.

Temperature was measured using a 10 k$\Omega$ negative temperature coefficient (NTC) thermistor embedded in the PASCO apparatus. The thermistor resistance was measured using a digital multimeter, and the relationship between resistance and temperature was established using the PASCO calibration table. The tabulated data provided discrete pairs $(R, T)$, which were interpolated using linear interpolation for intermediate values. During all three trials, the thermistor resistance ranged from 11200 to 11400 $\Omega$, corresponding to a temperature range of $T = 22.2$--22.5°C (nominal $T_{\text{nom}} = 22.35$°C). The interpolation uncertainty for temperatures within the calibration range is estimated at $\pm 0.1$°C; this propagates to an uncertainty of approximately $\pm 0.5\%$ in air viscosity.

Atmospheric pressure was measured using a calibrated barometer and recorded as $P = 75.9$ cmHg, equivalent to 101191 Pa (approximately 1.00 standard atmosphere). This value was used to calculate the air density via the ideal gas law:
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\text{air}} = \frac{PM}{RT}
\end{equation}
where $M = 0.02897$ kg/mol is the molar mass of air, $R = 8.314$ J/(mol·K) is the universal gas constant, and $T$ is the measured absolute temperature. At our measured conditions, this yields $\rho_{\text{air}} \approx 1.189$ kg/m$^3$, with an uncertainty of approximately $\pm 0.5\%$ dominated by the temperature uncertainty.

\subsection{Video Recording and Analysis Procedure}

High-speed video recordings of droplet motion were captured using a digital camera mounted to the observation telescope at a frame rate of approximately 16.68 fps (the standard frame rate for the camera used). Each trial consisted of two phases: pre-ionization and post-ionization.

For the initial measurement, the electric field was cycled between on and off states to induce sustained oscillation. The field was set to approximately 553.5 V (on state), causing the droplet to rise upward against gravity. Once the particle crossed one major reticle division ($0.5$ mm), the field was switched to zero volts (off state), allowing the droplet to fall purely under gravitational and viscous forces until it reached the starting position. This on-off cycling was repeated multiple times to produce a series of 5--7 complete oscillation cycles (alternating rise and fall phases), spanning approximately 30--40 seconds of continuous video recording. This procedure allowed both the upward velocity (field on) and downward velocity (field off) to be measured on the same droplet under consistent environmental conditions.

After completing Drop 1, an ionizer was activated for exactly 5 seconds to expose the droplet to a source of ionizing radiation, which can increase the total charge on the droplet. Immediately following the ionization period, Drop 2 was recorded on the same droplet, again using the same electric field cycling procedure: field on (causing upward motion), then field off (causing downward motion), repeated to capture 5--7 oscillation cycles at the new charge state.

The key advantage of this two-drop procedure is that both measurements are performed on the \\emph{same droplet}, eliminating mass variations that would otherwise introduce systematic errors. The change in charge due to ionization ($\Delta q = q_{Drop 2} - q_{Drop 1}$) can thus be directly related to the ionization process.

\subsection{Velocity Extraction from Video Frames}

The video files from each drop were analyzed via an interactive video player written in Python and OpenCV. This tool displays the video frame-by-frame and allows us to identify the precise frame numbers at which the droplet reaches its topmost and bottommost turning points.

For each identified pair of turning points, the following information was recorded:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Top and bottom frame numbers
    \item Time interval, which we computed as $\Delta t = (n_{\text{frame,bottom}} - n_{\text{frame,top}}) / f_{\text{rate}}$, where $f_{\text{rate}} = 16.68$ fps
\end{itemize}

The terminal velocity for each oscillation cycle is then calculated as:
\begin{equation}
v = \frac{\text{(reticle divisions)} \times 0.500 \text{ mm}}{\Delta t}
\end{equation}

Multiple cycles per droplet (typically 5--7) are averaged to obtain a mean velocity and standard deviation. The standard deviation serves as a quantitative measure of the velocity uncertainty due to frame-level timing jitter and reticle reading uncertainty.

Two features of the data are notable. First, the falling velocities are considerably more consistent than the rising velocities: fall velocity coefficients of variation (CV) are 3.8--11.5\%, while rise velocity CVs range from 4.5\% up to 48.2\%. This asymmetry is physically significant. The falling phase depends only on the droplet mass and air viscosity (both constant), so fall velocities should be---and are---stable. The rising phase depends additionally on the electric force $qE$, so any change in $q$ between oscillation cycles will manifest as a step change in $v_{\text{rise}}$. The large, non-random scatter in rise velocities (e.g., Trial~2, Drop~1, where individual rise velocities increase monotonically from 0.139 to 0.363~mm/s over six cycles) strongly suggests that stray ionization from ambient radiation altered the droplet charge \emph{during} the measurement window. These are not random fluctuations: they represent discrete changes in the integer number of elementary charges on the droplet.

Second, within each trial the pre- and post-ionization falling velocities agree to within 1--9\%, confirming that the two drops in each trial are the same physical droplet (and therefore the same mass). In Trial~1, the agreement is excellent (0.121 vs.\ 0.123~mm/s, 1.2\% difference), while Trials~2 and~3 show slightly larger differences (6.7\% and 9.2\%, respectively), possibly due to slow evaporation of the oil droplet over time or minor environmental drift.

\subsection{Sources of Uncertainty}

Random uncertainties arise from uncontrollable fluctuations in the experimental measurement. The following sources contribute to the variance in measured velocities:

The video camera records at approximately 16.68 fps, corresponding to a temporal resolution of $\Delta t_{\text{frame}} = 1/16.68 = 0.0600$~s per frame. When identifying turning points, we selected the frame at which the droplet momentarily stops (zero velocity). There is an uncertainty of $\pm 1$ frame in this identification, leading to a timing uncertainty at each endpoint of $\sigma_{t,\text{end}} = 1/f_{\text{rate}}$. Since both endpoints carry this uncertainty, the total timing uncertainty per interval is:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_t = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{f_{\text{rate}}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{16.68 \text{ fps}} \approx 0.0849 \text{ s}
\end{equation}

For a typical falling interval (e.g., Trial~2 Drop~1 with $\bar{t}_{\text{fall}} \approx 8.3$~s), the fractional timing uncertainty is $\sigma_t / \Delta t \approx 1.0\%$. For a faster rising interval (e.g., Trial~2 Drop~2 with $\bar{t}_{\text{rise}} \approx 1.6$~s), the fractional uncertainty is larger at $\sigma_t / \Delta t \approx 5.3\%$. This explains why faster oscillation phases have proportionally larger velocity uncertainties from timing alone.

The reticle scale visible in the video has major divisions of 0.500~mm. Each oscillation traverses one major division, and the estimated uncertainty in reading the droplet position at the turning points is $\pm 0.05$ divisions ($\pm 0.025$~mm), giving a fractional distance uncertainty of:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma_d}{d} = \frac{0.025 \text{ mm}}{0.5 \text{ mm}} = 5.0\%
\end{equation}

Since $v = d / \Delta t$, the fractional velocity uncertainty from timing and distance uncertainties combines in quadrature. The actual observed cycle-to-cycle variability (Tables~1--3) encompasses these effects along with additional physical sources such as thermal fluctuations, residual air currents, and stray charging. The standard error of the mean is:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\bar{v}} = \frac{\sigma_v}{\sqrt{N}}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of cycles (4--7). For example, Trial~3 Drop~2 with $N = 5$ rise cycles and $\sigma_v = 0.008$~mm/s yields $\sigma_{\bar{v}} = 0.003$~mm/s, while Trial~1 Drop~1 with $N = 5$ rise cycles and $\sigma_v = 0.084$~mm/s yields $\sigma_{\bar{v}} = 0.038$~mm/s, illustrating the large range in measurement precision across droplets.

Systematic uncertainties arise from calibration errors or apparatus limitations that shift all measurements in a consistent direction. The applied voltage was measured using a digital multimeter with a range of $V = 552$--$555$~V (nominal $553.5$~V), representing a fractional uncertainty of:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma_V}{V} = \frac{1.5 \text{ V}}{553.5 \text{ V}} \approx 0.27\%
\end{equation}

The thermistor temperature uncertainty of $\pm 0.15$°C propagates to an air viscosity uncertainty of:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\sigma_\eta}{\eta} \approx 0.5 \times \frac{0.15 \text{ K}}{295.5 \text{ K}} \approx 0.025\%
\end{equation}
which is negligible compared to velocity uncertainties. The plate separation $d = 7.37$~mm carries a manufacturer uncertainty of $\pm 0.02$~mm (0.27\%). The combined systematic contribution from voltage, temperature, and plate separation is propagated through the charge formula by evaluating the charge at the corners of the $(V_{\text{lo}}, V_{\text{hi}}) \times (T_{\text{lo}}, T_{\text{hi}})$ parameter space.

\section{Results and Analysis}

\subsection{Charge Determination}

The droplet radius is calculated from the falling velocity using:
\begin{equation}
a_1 = \sqrt{\frac{9\eta v_{\text{fall}}}{2g(\rho_{\text{oil}} - \rho_{\text{air}})}}
\end{equation}
and the Cunningham slip correction is applied:
\begin{equation}
q = \frac{q_0}{\left[1 + \frac{b}{p \, a_1}\right]^{3/2}}
\end{equation}
where $q_0$ is the uncorrected charge from the force balance and $b = 8.226 \times 10^{-3}$~Pa$\cdot$m is the Cunningham constant. The uncorrected charge is:
\begin{equation}
q_0 = \frac{18\pi d}{V} \sqrt{\frac{\eta^3 v_{\text{fall}}}{2(\rho_{\text{oil}} - \rho_{\text{air}})g}} \, (v_{\text{rise}} + v_{\text{fall}})
\end{equation}
with $V = 553.5 \pm 1.5$~V and $d = 7.37 \pm 0.02$~mm.

In the next subsection we relax this assumption and analyze each oscillation cycle independently.

\subsection{Per-Cycle Charge Analysis and Charge State Detection}

Because the falling velocity depends only on the droplet mass (constant), the mean fall velocity $\bar{v}_{\text{fall}}$ reliably determines the droplet radius $a_1$ regardless of any charge changes. For each individual rise cycle $i$, we compute a per-cycle charge:
\begin{equation}
q_i = \frac{q_{0,i}}{\left[1 + \frac{b}{p\,a_1}\right]^{3/2}}, \quad
q_{0,i} = \frac{18\pi d}{V}\sqrt{\frac{\eta^3 \bar{v}_{\text{fall}}}{2\Delta\rho\,g}}\,(v_{\text{rise},i} + \bar{v}_{\text{fall}})
\end{equation}
where $v_{\text{rise},i}$ is the rise velocity measured in cycle $i$ and all other quantities use the same values as before. If the droplet charge is truly constant, all $q_i$ should agree within noise; if the charge changes between cycles, $q_i$ will exhibit discrete jumps.

Consecutive cycles whose rise velocities differ by less than 15\% are grouped into a single \emph{charge state}. A step exceeding 15\% signals a transition to a new charge state. Table~5 presents all identified charge states across the six droplet recordings.

\subsection{Elementary Charge Determination}

Two approaches are used to extract $e$ from the data. The first (\emph{mean-based}) uses the original 6 mean charges from Table~4, while the second (\emph{charge-state-based}) uses all 20 identified charge states from Table~5.

\subsubsection{Mean-Based Estimate}

For each droplet, the per-droplet estimate is $e_i = q_i / n_i$ with uncertainty $\delta e_i = \delta q_i / n_i$. The weighted average (with weights $w_i = 1/\delta e_i^2$) yields:
\begin{equation}
e_{\text{mean}} = (1.603 \pm 0.051) \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}
\end{equation}
This agrees with the CODATA 2018 value to within 0.05\%, well within the 3.2\% measurement uncertainty. However, as discussed above, this approach averages over charge states that changed during the recording.

\subsubsection{Charge-State-Based Estimate}

Using the 20 charge states from the per-cycle analysis, each charge state yields an independent estimate $e_j = \bar{q}_j / n_j$. States with multiple cycles provide an uncertainty from the standard deviation of $q$ within the state; single-cycle states are assigned a conservative 10\% fractional uncertainty. The weighted average gives:
\begin{equation}
e_{\text{states}} = (1.554 \pm 0.001) \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}
\end{equation}
corresponding to a 3.0\% deviation from the CODATA value. This is slightly less accurate than the mean-based result, likely because many charge states contain only 1--2 cycles, leading to less precise individual charge estimates whose systematic biases (e.g., from using the mean $v_{\text{fall}}$ across all cycles) are not averaged away.

Figure~\ref{fig:per_drop_e} shows the per-charge-state estimates, with all 20 data points spanning a wider range than the 6 mean-based estimates but still clustering around the accepted value.

\subsubsection{Transition-Based Estimate}

The charge differences between consecutive charge states within a droplet provide a third, independent estimate of $e$. If a droplet transitions from $n_1$ to $n_2$ elementary charges, then $e \approx |\Delta q| / |\Delta n|$. Across all droplets, 14 transitions were identified, yielding:
\begin{equation}
e_{\text{trans}} = (1.72 \pm 0.45) \times 10^{-19} \text{ C}
\end{equation}
This estimate has the largest uncertainty (26\%) because each transition involves only 2 charge states, and the charge differences amplify relative errors. Nonetheless, it provides an independent confirmation that the charge changes are consistent with integer multiples of $e$.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Assessment of Experimental Precision and Accuracy}

Our mean-based result of $e = (1.603 \pm 0.051) \times 10^{-19}$~C agrees with the CODATA value to within 0.05\%, which is well within the 3.2\% measurement uncertainty. This level of \emph{accuracy} exceeded our expectations given the relatively crude nature of the velocity measurements: manual frame-by-frame identification of turning points at only 16.68~fps, with each oscillation traversing just one 0.500~mm reticle division. The agreement is partly fortuitous---the weighted average benefits from partial cancellation of positive and negative deviations across the six droplets---but it nonetheless confirms that the Millikan oil drop method is robust even with modest instrumentation.

The \emph{precision}, however, is poor by modern standards. The 3.2\% fractional uncertainty on $e$ is five orders of magnitude worse than the CODATA value (exact by definition since 2019). Individual droplet charge uncertainties range from 4.8\% (T3D2) to 13.6\% (T2D1), with an average of 9.7\%. This wide range is itself informative: T3D2 maintained a single stable charge state throughout its recording ($n = 12$, CV of rise velocities = 4.5\%), while T2D1 underwent four charge state transitions ($n = 4 \to 5 \to 7 \to 8$, CV of rise velocities = 41.1\%). The per-cycle charge analysis confirms that most of the ``velocity scatter'' reported in the uncertainty budget is not random noise at all---it is a physical signal of mid-oscillation charge changes.

The measurement is overwhelmingly dominated by velocity scatter, which accounts for $> 99.9\%$ of the total variance. Systematic uncertainties from the applied voltage ($0.27\%$) and temperature ($0.05\%$) are negligible by comparison. This has a clear implication: \emph{no improvement to the voltage supply, thermometry, or plate separation measurement would meaningfully improve the result} at the current level of velocity precision. The path to better results runs entirely through the velocity measurement.

\subsection{Effect of Increased Measurement Time}

If the droplet charge were truly constant during a recording, the statistical uncertainty on the mean velocity would scale as $\sigma_{\bar{v}} = \sigma_v / \sqrt{N}$, where $N$ is the number of oscillation cycles. Since $\delta q / q \propto \sigma_{\bar{v}} / \bar{v}$, doubling the number of cycles (from $\sim 5$ to $\sim 10$, roughly doubling the recording time from $\sim 35$~s to $\sim 70$~s) would reduce the statistical uncertainty by a factor of $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.4$, bringing the average per-droplet uncertainty from $\sim 9.7\%$ to $\sim 6.9\%$ and the final uncertainty on $e$ from $3.2\%$ to $\sim 2.3\%$.

However, our per-cycle analysis reveals a fundamental complication: 5 of 6 droplets experienced charge state changes within $\sim 30$~s of recording. Longer recordings would capture \emph{more} charge transitions, not fewer, and naively averaging over a longer time series would mix more charge states together, potentially \emph{worsening} the accuracy of the mean-based estimate. The $\sqrt{N}$ improvement assumes independent, identically distributed measurements---an assumption that is violated when the underlying charge is changing.

The correct approach for extended recordings is the per-cycle charge analysis developed in Section~4.2: resolve each charge state individually, compute $q/n$ per state, and weight by the number of cycles in each state. Under this framework, longer recordings \emph{would} help, because more cycles per charge state would reduce the per-state uncertainty, and more observed transitions would provide additional independent estimates of $e$ via the $|\Delta q|/|\Delta n|$ method. The key is that the analysis must account for charge state changes rather than blindly averaging.

\subsection{Improving Velocity Measurement Precision}

The dominant velocity uncertainty has two components: timing resolution and position resolution.

\textbf{Timing resolution.} At 16.68~fps, the $\pm 1$~frame uncertainty at each turning point gives a per-interval timing uncertainty of $\sigma_t = \sqrt{2}/f_{\text{rate}} \approx 85$~ms. For a fast rise interval ($\sim 1.6$~s in T2D2), this corresponds to a 5.3\% fractional uncertainty---comparable to the total velocity scatter for the best droplets. Recording at 60~fps (readily achievable with modern USB cameras) would reduce this to $\sigma_t \approx 24$~ms, a factor of 3.6 improvement. At 120~fps, $\sigma_t \approx 12$~ms, approaching the regime where other error sources become relevant. Higher frame rates would also make turning point identification less ambiguous, reducing the systematic component of timing error.

\textbf{Position resolution.} Each oscillation traverses one reticle division (0.500~mm), with an estimated position uncertainty of $\pm 0.025$~mm (5.0\% fractional). This could be improved by (a) traversing multiple reticle divisions per oscillation, which increases$~d$ and reduces the fractional distance error, or (b) using sub-pixel tracking algorithms to determine the droplet centroid position with higher precision than visual inspection allows. Modern centroid-fitting algorithms routinely achieve sub-pixel accuracy ($< 0.1$ pixel), which, combined with appropriate magnification, could reduce the position uncertainty by an order of magnitude.

\textbf{Automated tracking.} Replacing manual frame-by-frame annotation with automated droplet tracking (e.g., via background subtraction and centroid fitting on every frame) would eliminate the turning-point identification paradigm entirely. Instead of measuring $v = d/\Delta t$ from two discrete points, one could fit the entire position-time trajectory to extract velocities from a continuous kinematic model. This would use all available frames, not just turning points, and would suppress the effect of any single misidentified frame. Combined with higher frame rates, automated tracking could plausibly reduce velocity uncertainties to $< 1\%$, at which point systematic errors in voltage and temperature would begin to matter.

\subsection{Mitigating Mid-Oscillation Charge Changes}

The most physically interesting source of error is the stray ionization that changes the droplet charge during recording. Our per-cycle analysis identifies these events after the fact, but two experimental modifications could reduce their impact:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Shielding the apparatus.} The charge changes are caused by ambient ionizing radiation (cosmic rays, natural radioactivity) creating ion pairs near the droplet. Enclosing the apparatus in a lead-lined enclosure or performing the experiment in a low-background environment would reduce the rate of stray ionization events, allowing longer uninterrupted charge-state plateaus and more precise per-state charge measurements.
    \item \textbf{Faster oscillation cycling.} Reducing the time per oscillation cycle (by using a stronger electric field or smaller droplets) would allow more cycles to be completed before a charge change occurs. If a typical charge change happens every $\sim 10$~s (as suggested by our data), and each cycle takes $\sim 5$~s, we capture only $\sim 2$ cycles per charge state on average. Reducing the cycle time to $\sim 1$~s would yield $\sim 10$ cycles per state, greatly improving per-state statistics.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Alternative Experimental Approaches}

Several modifications to the basic Millikan experiment could yield substantially better results:

\textbf{Balanced-drop method.} Rather than measuring both rise and fall velocities, one can adjust the voltage to \emph{suspend} the droplet motionless ($v = 0$), so that $qE = mg$ exactly. This eliminates the velocity measurement entirely and determines $q$ directly from the suspension voltage. The precision then depends on how accurately the voltage can be tuned and how stably the droplet can be held, rather than on velocity measurements.

\textbf{Single-electron sensitivity.} Using droplets with very small charges ($n = 1$ or $2$) would simplify the quantization analysis, since the integer assignment becomes unambiguous. This requires selecting very slowly rising droplets (small $q$) and demands higher velocity precision to resolve the correspondingly small rise velocities, but it eliminates the difficulty of assigning large quantum numbers where the fractional spacing between adjacent $n$ values is small (e.g., $q/e = 12.01$ vs.\ 13 is a 7.6\% difference, while $q/e = 1.0$ vs.\ 2 is a 50\% difference).

\textbf{Modern detector technology.} Replacing the optical microscope with a CCD/CMOS camera system with calibrated magnification and automated centroid tracking, combined with a high-stability voltage source ($\delta V / V < 0.01\%$), digital temperature control, and environmental shielding, could plausibly achieve per-droplet charge uncertainties of $< 0.5\%$, approaching the systematic floor set by uncertainties in the oil density and Cunningham slip correction constants.
